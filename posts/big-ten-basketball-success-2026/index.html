Every March the same debate dominates the college sports scene, which conference is truly the best? The Big 12 consistently has a gauntlet of a regular season, and the SEC prides itself on having top tier athletes, but these narritives don’t win championships. When you take away the loud, often misleading, voices and take a look at the numbers from the 2025-2026 season, reality shows its true colors. The Big Ten didn’t just participate in this historic season, they dominated it.

The B1G set a completely new standard this year, proving they were better than the SEC, the Big 12, and everyone else contending for the title.

The Non-Conference Start: Winning by Margins

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(hoopR)
library(patchwork)
library(ggrepel)


# downloaded hoopR information to make it easier when running the code chunk over time
bball_data <- read_csv("data/ncaa_mbb.csv")


# get the conference data to join, data from sports reference
conference <- read_csv("data/teams_conferences.csv")


# select only what I need
conference <- conference |>
  select(School, Conf, SOS)


# join bball with conference
bball_data <- bball_data |>
  left_join(conference, by = c("team_location" = "School"))


# filter for non conference games
non_conf_bball <- bball_data |>
  #filter for games before conference play since conference play differentials won't work
  filter(game_date < as.Date("2026-01-01"))


# time for the actual first chart I have
chart1_data <- non_conf_bball |>
  left_join(conference, by = c("opponent_team_location" = "School"), suffix = c("", "_opp")) |>
  # filter to ensure only non conference games are considered
  filter(Conf != Conf_opp | is.na(Conf_opp)) |>
  mutate(
    score_diff = team_score - opponent_team_score,
    date_of_game = as.Date(game_date),
  ) |>
  group_by(Conf, date_of_game) |>
  summarize(
    avg_score_diff = mean(score_diff, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  )


chart1_data_bigten <- chart1_data |>
  filter(Conf == "Big Ten")

chart1_data_sec <- chart1_data |>
  filter(Conf == "SEC")

chart1_data_bigeast <- chart1_data |>
  filter(Conf == "Big East")


ggplot() +
  geom_line(
    data = chart1_data,
    aes(x = date_of_game, y = avg_score_diff, group = Conf),
    color = "grey",
    alpha = 0.5,
    linewidth = 2) +
  geom_line(
    data = chart1_data_sec,
    aes(x = date_of_game, y = avg_score_diff, group = Conf),
    color = "red3",
    alpha = 0.2,
    linewidth = 1.5) +
  geom_line(
    data = chart1_data_bigeast,
    aes(x = date_of_game, y = avg_score_diff, group = Conf),
    color = "navy",
    alpha = 0.2,
    linewidth = 1.5) +
  geom_line(
    data = chart1_data_bigten,
    aes(x = date_of_game, y = avg_score_diff, group = Conf),
    color = "deepskyblue3",
    linewidth = 1.5) +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0, color = "black") +
  annotate(
    "text", x = as.Date("2025-12-12"), y = 33, label = "Big Ten", color = "deepskyblue3", fontface = "bold", size = 4
  ) +
  annotate(
    "text", x = as.Date("2025-12-07"), y = -37, label = "SEC", color = "red3", fontface = "bold", size = 4
  ) +
  annotate(
    "text", x = as.Date("2025-11-25"), y = -32, label = "Big East", color = "navy", fontface = "bold", size = 4
  ) +
  theme_minimal() +
  labs(
    title = "The Big Ten consistently outscored opponents",
    subtitle = "Among all conferences, the B1G stayed steadily above the average for point differential.",
    x = "Game Date",
    y = "Score Differentials",
    caption = "By: Jake Ostdiek | Source: SportsDataverse for R & SportsReference"
  ) +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(size = 16, face = "bold"),
    axis.title = element_text(size = 8),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size = 10),
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
    plot.title.position = "plot"
  )

To find the true strength of a conference, you have to first look at the non-conference competition that starts off every season. As soon as January hits, conference rivals go at it, dragging down overall conference metrics. But in the non-conference slate? That is where you see what each conference is really made of.

When we look at all games played before January 1st, 2026 (Average start date for conference games), the Big Ten teases its dominance.

The data reveals a consistent trend - The Big Ten wasn’t just winning games, they were dismantling defenses and locking down offenses. Looking at the average scoring differentials, the B1G separated itself from the pack early. While other conferences hovered near the baseline of “just okay”, the Big Ten consistently was outscoring their opponents by significant margins. This established a level of dominance before conference play was even a worry.

The Test of Resilience: Strength of Schedule

Code 
# find number of teams per conference
conf_teams <- conference |>
  group_by(Conf) |>
  summarize(
    num_teams = n(),
    .groups = "drop"
  )


# chart 2 is strength of schedule for the top 10 conferences
chart2_data <- non_conf_bball |>
  # group_by summarise to average each conferences SOS
  group_by(Conf) |>
  summarize(
    avg_conf_sos = mean(SOS, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) |>
  left_join(conf_teams, by = "Conf") |>
  arrange(desc(avg_conf_sos)) |>
  head(10)


chart2_data_bigten <- chart2_data |>
  filter(Conf == "Big Ten")

chart2_data_sec <- chart2_data |>
  filter(Conf == "SEC")

chart2_data_bigeast <- chart2_data |>
  filter(Conf == "Big East")

The immediate counterargument for non-conference blowouts usually sounds like this, “Oh yeah, name one tough opponent you’ve played.” That’s fair to ask of someone prideful in their conference. Padding the stat sheet against mid-majors before the new year has become something of a college basketball tradition.

But the Big Ten wasn’t hiding from anyone.

When mapping the average Strength of Schedule (SOS) across the top conferences, the Big Ten stands its ground at the top. They didn’t just rack up high point differentials against non-conference teams, they did it against the toughest competition in the country as well. This wasn’t the product of a few outstanding teams carrying the rest of the conference either. From top to bottom, the conference was ruthless, ensuring any team that came out of the Big Ten regular season was battle-tested and ready for a fight.

Regular season metrics are great, but the NCAA Tournament is a lie detector test disguised as college basketball tournament. If a conference is truly the best, it has to be able to continue their regular-season hype into reaffirming tournament success.

To measure this, we don’t justlook at how many bids a conference received, we look at efficiency. How successfully did a conference’s total number of teams survive from round to round?

# round of 64 teams
r64_data <- tourney_data |>
  distinct(Conf, team_location) |>

    r64_count = n(),

# round of 32 by filtering game dates
r32_data <- tourney_data |>

    r32_count = n(),
    .groups = "drop"

    s16_count = n(),

The conversion rates and number of teams advanced tells the final, undeniable piece of the story. While the SEC, Big 12 and others saw their representation fall off in the opening weekend, the Big Ten stayed true to what they knew, and won. From the Round of 64 to the Sweet 16, the Big Ten advanced 66.67% of their teams successfully while the SEC advanced 40% and the Big 12 advanced 37.5%. The Big Ten added a cherry on top and broke a conference record for number of teams that advanced to the Sweet 16, with 6 teams advancing (previously 4).

The success doesn’t stop there. From the Sweet 16 to the Elite Eight, the Big Ten was the only conference to advance more than one team to the Elite Eight, and they advanced four, breaking yet another conference record for number of teams in the Elite Eight (previously three). The high percentage of B1G teams surviving the early rounds of chaos proves that their regular season metrics weren’t by chance. They were prepared for the battlefield that is March Madness because their entire season trained to show up when it mattered most.

The Big Ten’s Final Statement: The Championship

You can have the best point differentials and the highest SOS, but without a banner to raise, the argument quickly falls apart. The Big Ten answered that with a exclamation point, with Michigan ultimately cutting down the nets to win it all.

The 2025-2026 season wasn’t up to debate. From the opening tip-off of the non-conference schedule to the final buzzer of the National Championship game, the data tells the exact same story basketball enjoyers watched all season. The Big Ten is the king of college basketball.
